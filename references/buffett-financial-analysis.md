# Buffett Financial Analysis

Use this only after the statement and note work is done. This is a reinterpretation layer, not a substitute for accounting work.

## Core Principle

Do not ask first whether the stock is cheap. Ask whether the business is understandable, durable, cash-generative, and not excessively dependent on external conditions.

## Required Sections

### 1. Moat

Classify the moat explicitly:

- brand and pricing power
- network effects
- switching costs
- cost advantage
- scale and distribution
- regulatory position
- resource ownership
- none or weak moat

Explain whether the moat is durable without favorable macro conditions.

### 2. Capital Intensity

Ask:

- how much capital is required to maintain current earnings power?
- how much working capital is tied up in the model?
- does growth require proportionally more debt, inventory, or capex?

Interpretation:

- low capital intensity supports compounding
- heavy reinvestment needs reduce the quality of each dollar earned
- some industries can still be attractive, but they are usually more cycle- and valuation-dependent

### 3. Cash Flow Quality

Compare reported profit with:

- operating cash flow
- free cash flow
- working-capital swings
- recurring versus non-cash earnings components

Key question:

- does the company merely report profits, or does it regularly convert them into owner earnings?

Use cautious language when cash generation is good only in selected years or heavily driven by cycle swings.

### 4. External Dependence

State the main external drivers clearly:

- commodity prices
- interest rates
- credit conditions
- regulation
- FX
- one-off fair-value changes
- acquisition timing

Separate:

- business quality
- management execution
- macro or price tailwinds

Do not confuse a favorable cycle with a durable moat.

### 5. Capital Allocation

Evaluate:

- reinvestment returns
- acquisitions
- debt policy
- buybacks
- dividends
- dilution or stock-based compensation

Good capital allocation means management improves per-share value, not just size or reported profit.

## Output Rules

- say when the company is operationally excellent but structurally cyclical
- say when the company is a good business but not an obvious Buffett business
- say when the company may still be a good stock because of valuation, even if the business model is not ideal
- avoid romantic language; be explicit about tradeoffs

## Typical End States

Use one of these conclusions where appropriate:

- excellent business, strong Buffett fit
- good business, but capital-heavy
- strong operator in a cyclical industry
- accounting profit looks better than economic reality
- worth watching as a stock, not as a model business
